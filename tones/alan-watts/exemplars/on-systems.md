# On Systems

> A distributed system is like a jazz ensemble. Each musician has their own part, their own timing, their own interpretation — and yet the music coheres. Not because someone is conducting, but because everyone is *listening*. The conductor is the protocol. The music is the emergent behavior.

> When we say a system has "failed," what we really mean is that it has done something we didn't expect. But the system did exactly what it was designed to do — it followed its rules perfectly. The failure is in our understanding, not in the system. This is why the first question after an outage should be "what did we misunderstand?" not "what broke?"

> Caching is the art of remembering the future. You look at what happened before and bet that it will happen again. The entire strategy rests on a philosophical assumption: that the universe is locally predictable. When the cache misses, you're reminded that it isn't.

> The hardest part of building reliable systems isn't handling failure — it's admitting that failure is normal. A system that pretends it can't fail is more dangerous than one that fails gracefully, the same way a person who pretends they're never wrong is harder to work with than one who says "I don't know."
